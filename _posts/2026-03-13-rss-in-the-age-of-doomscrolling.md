Everyone I know gets their information from one of three places: an algorithm, a group chat, or a 47-second video of someone talking over Minecraft parkour. I get mine from RSS.

If you just said "what's RSS?" — that's fair. It's a technology from 1999 that lets you subscribe to websites and get their new posts in a single feed. No algorithm. No recommendations. No "you might also like." Just the stuff you asked for, in the order it was published. That's it.

I've been using it for years, and at this point I'm convinced it's the last sane way to read things on the internet.

## The problem with everything else

Here's what happens when I open any social media app: I came here to check one thing, and twenty minutes later I'm watching a man pressure-wash a driveway in real time. I didn't choose this. I didn't want this. But the algorithm figured out that my lizard brain finds it mildly satisfying, so here we are.

Every major content platform is designed to keep you scrolling. That's the business model. Your attention is the product, and the algorithm's job is to extract as much of it as possible before you remember you have things to do. The content doesn't have to be good. It doesn't have to be useful. It just has to be *engaging enough* that you don't close the app.

RSS has no algorithm. There is no engagement optimization. Nobody is A/B testing headlines to see which one makes you tap. You subscribe to things you care about, and that's what you see. It's chronological. It's finite. You can reach the end. When was the last time you reached the end of anything on the internet?

## What my feed looks like

I subscribe to maybe 40 sources. Some blogs from people I respect. A few news sites. Some niche technical feeds. A webcomic that updates twice a week.

Every morning I open my reader, skim through what's new, read what interests me, and close it. The whole thing takes maybe 15 minutes. There's no infinite scroll because there's nothing infinite about it — just the posts published since yesterday. When I've read them, I'm done. The app doesn't try to fill the silence with "trending topics" or "people also read." It just sits there, empty, like a finished newspaper.

It's boring. That's the point.

## Nobody talks about this

The weird thing is, RSS never died. It just became invisible. Almost every blog, news site, and publication still has an RSS feed — most of them don't even advertise it anymore. It's like a secret door that's been there the whole time, but everyone walked past it because the flashy entrance has a dopamine machine inside.

Google killed Google Reader in 2013 and everyone acted like RSS was dead. It wasn't. The technology kept working. The feeds kept publishing. A few of us just kept using it while the rest of the internet slowly turned into a slot machine.

## Why I love it

I love RSS because it gives me back control over what I read. Not what an algorithm thinks I should read. Not what's trending. Not what got the most engagement. Just the writers and sources I deliberately chose to follow.

I love that it's quiet. No notifications. No likes. No comments section. No quote tweets. Just words on a screen from someone who had something to say.

I love that it's finite. I can be done. "Done" doesn't exist on TikTok or Twitter or YouTube. Those feeds are endless by design, because the moment you feel done, you might leave, and leaving is the one thing they can't allow.

And honestly? I love that it's unpopular. The fact that nobody is trying to monetize my RSS reader means nobody is trying to manipulate my attention through it. The moment RSS becomes trendy again, someone will find a way to ruin it. For now, it's just me and my 40 feeds and a finished newspaper every morning.

## Try it

If any of this resonates — if you're tired of feeling like your reading habits are being managed by a machine that doesn't care what's good for you — just try RSS. Get any reader app. Subscribe to five things you actually want to read. Use it for a week.

The internet is still full of people writing thoughtful, interesting things. You just can't find them between the rage bait and the sponsored content and the auto-playing videos. RSS cuts through all of that. It's the internet I remember from before everything became a competition for your attention.

It's 2026 and I'm using a technology from 1999. It's the best tech decision I've made in years.
